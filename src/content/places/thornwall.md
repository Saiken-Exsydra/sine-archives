## The Ridge

The Thornwall is not a mountain range in the conventional sense. It is a series of ancient, exposed **metamorphic rock fins** that protrude from the highland plateau in long, parallel ridgelines running east-west — each several hundred meters tall but generally less than a kilometer wide at the base.

The formations were created by the same curvature-stress geology that characterizes the Empire's deeper geological history: folded upward over millions of years by Vael'Khar's gravitational gradient, then stripped of their softer surrounding rock by glacial action. What remains is the hard core — dark grey and rust-red banded stone, standing in narrow vertical walls like the teeth of something buried.

The result is a landscape of extraordinary visual drama: tall, narrow ridges of banded rock, separated by narrow valleys of acidic soil and wind-carved shrubbery. The Thornwall stretches approximately **800 km** along the northern edge of the Central Region, constituting a significant geographic boundary between the temperate central lowlands and the cold highland zone.

## The Crossings

The major passes through the Thornwall are fewer and narrower than those of the Hal'vorn Ridge System further north, and their approaches are more dramatic.

The **Imperial Spine** crosses the Thornwall through the **Krel Tunnel** — an 18-kilometer bored passage that took over twenty years to complete and remains one of the great engineering achievements of imperial rail construction. The tunnel carries the full freight and passenger volume of the Empire's primary rail artery through the ridge system, connecting the Central lowlands to the Northern Region without requiring the rail line to climb the ridges themselves.

Travellers crossing via surface road use the **Thornwall Pass** — a winding route through the widest gap in the system. On clear High Radiance days, the pass provides one of the most cited scenic views in the Empire: the red-and-grey fins of the Thornwall above, and the green Central lowlands spread below as far as the eye can reach.

## The Boundary

The Thornwall is a boundary in more than the geographic sense. North of it, the climate shifts — colder, wetter, the conifer forests beginning, the highland rivers running fast and cold. South of it, the Central Region opens into its temperate lowlands — the agricultural heartland, the river plains, the population centers. The Thornwall marks the transition as visibly as any feature in the Empire, and crossing it — whether by tunnel, by pass, or by the narrow valley routes that local communities have maintained for generations — is the moment when the north becomes the north and the center becomes the center.
