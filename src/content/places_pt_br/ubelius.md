## The River Crossing

Ube'lius is a large commercial city positioned at the confluence of two major river systems in the Central Region, approximately 1,500 km east of Kalt'sen along the band. Its geographic position made it a critical trading hub centuries before imperial consolidation, and it has retained that role as the **primary commercial node of the eastern Central Region**.

The city is low-lying, built across both banks of the river confluence and connected by a series of **stone bridges** that have been rebuilt and expanded over generations. The bridges are functional landmarks — each one carrying a name, a history, and a specific role in the city's daily traffic. The oldest are early consolidation-era stone constructions, wide enough to carry modern vehicle traffic and maintained continuously since their original building.

Population: approximately **1.2 million**.

---

## The Floods

Ube'lius floods periodically during peak Low Radiance snowmelt seasons, when the highland rivers run full and the confluence rises beyond its normal banks. The lower districts are built accordingly — ground floors elevated, doors heavy, cellars sealed. The people of Ube'lius do not regard the floods as disasters. They regard them as the price of living at the most commercially valuable geographic position in the eastern Empire, and they have been paying it long enough that the architecture has incorporated the cost.

---

## The Trading Culture

Ube'lius is a city where commerce is the dominant social grammar. The market districts are older than the imperial administrative quarter. The trading houses predate the garrison. The city's relationship with Kalt'sen is one of functional deference — Ube'lius acknowledges the capital's authority — combined with a commercial confidence that comes from knowing that a significant fraction of the Empire's internal trade passes through its bridges and its warehouses.

Visitors from the capital consistently note the difference in manner: Ube'lius is louder, faster, less concerned with institutional formality, and more concerned with whether the contract is signed and the cargo is moving. This is not a deficiency. It is a specialization.
